Thanksgiving: The National Day of Mourning

"In 1637 the Pequot Massacre took place, when over 700 Native men, women and children were slaughtered in what's now known as Mystic, Connecticut. The following day, the governor declared a day of thanksfiving and held a feast to celebrate their victories in battle. Thirty-nine years later in Massachuetts, the colonists declared a day of public celebration and thanksgiving, saying, there now scarce reamains a name or family of them 'Natives' but are either slain, captivated or fled, right after the slaughtering of a tribe including the beheading of Wampanoag chief Metacom, which reamined on display for 24 years after King Philip's war. It was not unitl 1863 that Abraham Lincoln officially declared it a national holiday, in the same year the Sioux were being removed from Minnesota, during which the bounty for a Sioux scalp was $25." - Allen Salway, Paper Magazine
